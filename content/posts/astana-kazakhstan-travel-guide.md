<div class="article-body">
<p><strong>Astana Kazakhstan</strong>, the capital of capitals, or <strong>Nur-Sultan</strong>, the city of ten names, boulevards with five lanes in each direction, and a shockingly low number of people on the street (coming as I was from overcrowded <strong>China</strong>). It felt something like <strong>Valencia</strong> or <strong>Brasilia</strong> with their architecture, and a little like <strong>Tbilisi</strong> or <strong>Baku</strong>.</p>

<h2>From China into the Republic of Kazakhstan</h2>

<p>It was a relief to cross from <strong>Urumqi</strong>, <strong>China</strong>, into <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>: unrestricted web access, people who also spoke English, food that suited my taste, and ideal weather at 25–28°C.</p>

<p>At first glance the city struck me as very vast, with wide boulevards, buildings spread far apart, developed and modern. I had ended up exactly where I needed to be.</p>

<h2>Things to do in Astana, Kazakhstan</h2>

<p><strong>Astana Kazakhstan</strong>, known as <strong>Nur-Sultan</strong> between 2019 and 2022, became the capital of <strong>Kazakhstan</strong> in 1997, relocating from <strong>Almaty</strong>, the country's former administrative center.</p>

<p><strong>Kazakhstan</strong>'s first president, <strong>Nursultan Nazarbayev</strong>, initiated the move from <strong>Almaty</strong> to <strong>Astana</strong> to support the economic development of the country's north. In 2019 the city was renamed <strong>Nur-Sultan</strong> in his honor, before they reversed course in 2022 and renamed it <strong>Astana</strong> once again, a name that simply means capital. The capital of capitals.</p>

<p><strong>Astana</strong> is obsessed with futuristic architecture. The whole city is built around geometric shapes and buildings with surprising forms. I was staying near the <strong>Kazakh Eli Monument</strong>, so that is where I started, among vast open spaces with very few passersby and a great deal of white. I liked it.</p>

<p>Because <strong>Astana</strong> is a new city, the important areas connect to one another with a certain urban logic to them, no scattered fragments. The center can be explored on foot, even if it stretches a few kilometers from one end to the other.</p>

<p>What can you do in <strong>Astana</strong>? Quite a lot for two or three days: the main sights are concentrated along the city's central axis and can be combined without effort into a single walking circuit.</p>

<h3>Palace of Peace and Reconciliation</h3>

<p>The <strong>Palace of Peace and Reconciliation</strong> is a pyramid designed by architect <strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Foster,_Baron_Foster_of_Thames_Bank" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Norman Foster</a></strong>, but it already felt dated to me, despite having been built in 2006. The surrounding park is more construction site than anything else; I picked my way through ditches just to photograph the <strong>Presidential Palace</strong> on the other side of the river.</p>

<h3>Baiterek Tower, Symbol of Astana</h3>

<p>The main attraction in <strong>Astana</strong> is <strong>Baiterek Tower</strong>, which you can climb for a panoramic view. The tower represents a tree holding a golden egg, inspired by a Kazakh legend about the mythical tree of life and the bird of happiness, <strong>Samruk</strong>.</p>

<p>If you walk past <strong>Baiterek Tower in Astana</strong>, you reach <strong>Khan Shatyr</strong>, another project by <strong>Norman Foster</strong>. It looks like a giant tent and houses a very large shopping center.</p>

<p>Across the river you'll also notice the <strong>Abu Dhabi Plaza</strong>, a cluster of towers that includes the tallest building in <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>. It's mostly office and residential space, not a tourist attraction per se, but it's hard to miss on the skyline and worth photographing from the riverbank near the <strong>Presidential Palace</strong>.</p>

<h3>The Botanical Garden</h3>

<p>The next day I headed toward the <strong>Botanical Garden</strong>. When I got there I found a large park with grass and small trees and no <strong>Botanical Garden</strong> in sight. It turned out the entire garden consisted of a few rows of pansies next to an arch. That was genuinely it. They probably wanted a <strong>Botanical Garden</strong> for their new city, and that was the best they could manage. 🙂</p>

<h3>Nur-Astana Grand Mosque</h3>

<p>The <strong>Nur-Astana Grand Mosque</strong> is one of the largest mosques in <strong>Central Asia</strong>, with a capacity of five thousand worshippers and four minarets rising 63 meters each. It's free to visit outside prayer times. Cover your shoulders and bring something for your head if you're a woman.</p>

<h3>Nur Alem Museum</h3>

<p>The <em>nouveau riche</em> urge to impress is endearing; the result is something like an amusement park. The <strong>Nur Alem Museum</strong> is shaped like a sphere and has eight floors you can climb for yet another panoramic view of the city. It makes for a pleasant science museum if you have children with you, but the upper floors were unbearably hot and I didn't stay long.</p>

<p><strong>Useful to know:</strong> admission to <strong>Baiterek Tower</strong> costs <strong>2,000 tenge</strong>, and the <strong>Nur Alem Museum</strong> <strong>1,500 tenge</strong>.</p>

<h3>Astana Opera</h3>

<p>If you're there long enough and have any interest in classical music, the <strong>Astana Opera</strong> is worth checking. Opened in 2013, it looks exactly as you'd expect a brand-new opera house in a brand-new capital to look: very grand, a little unearned, and remarkably well-programmed. Tickets are cheap by any <strong>European</strong> standard.</p>

<h2>Kazakhstan on a First Visit</h2>

<p><strong>Kazakhstan</strong>: 70% of the population is Muslim, but it's a tolerant form of Islam. Older women wear headscarves, sometimes younger married women too. I didn't see a burqa anywhere, but in <strong>Astana</strong> people also walk around in shorts.</p>

<p>At the hostel, a girl from <strong>Kazakhstan</strong> asked where I was from.<br>
"<strong>Romania</strong>"<br>
"Oh, wow, and you came from there...?"<br>
"Not directly, but yes"</p>

<p>She told me tourism isn't developed in <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>, which is why she asked. From what I saw on the streets, foreign tourists are very few, but that makes <strong>Astana</strong> all the more interesting. There's no crowding anywhere. And many locals speak English, at least at a <em>basic</em> level.</p>

<h2>Food in Astana, Kazakhstan</h2>

<p>Supermarkets carry products you'd find in <strong>Romania</strong> too, with small differences. At <strong>Small</strong>, for example, they made pancakes every morning, which seem very popular in <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>.</p>

<p>Their cuisine is meat-based, so it's not unusual to see horse on the menu, alongside lamb, beef, or camel.</p>

<h2>Getting to Astana</h2>
<p><strong>Astana International Airport</strong> has direct connections to the main <strong>Central Asian</strong> hubs and to a handful of <strong>European</strong> cities, mostly through <strong>Air Astana</strong> or <strong>FlyArystan</strong>, its low-cost subsidiary. From <strong>Europe</strong> you'll usually connect through <strong>Istanbul</strong>, <strong>Dubai</strong>, or <strong>Frankfurt</strong>. I had a flight from <strong>Urumqi</strong>, <strong>China</strong>.</p>
<p>If you're already in <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>, the <strong>Almaty to Astana</strong> route is well-covered: there are frequent flights (around an hour) and a train that takes roughly 12–14 hours overnight, comfortable and cheap. Most people fly, but the train is worth it if you're not in a hurry and want to see the steppe from the window.</p>

<h2>Where to Stay in Astana</h2>

<p>I stayed at <strong><a href="https://www.booking.com/hotel/kz/sulu-hostel-nur-sultan.html?aid=1266664&amp;label=postbooking_confemail;pbsource=conf_email_hotel_name" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Sulu Hostel</a></strong>. Eight nights in a small private room came to <strong>100 USD</strong>, small meaning windowless, but with access to a <em>living-coworking</em> space and a large kitchen. I felt comfortable there: modern, clean, good internet.</p>

<p>For getting around I used taxis ordered through the <strong>Yandex</strong> app. I never figured out where to buy bus tickets. I bought a SIM card from <strong>Beeline</strong> (<strong>7,000 tenge</strong> for a month, <strong>68 lei</strong>) to have mobile internet and a number to receive SMS messages and verify my <strong>Yandex</strong> account.</p>

<p><strong>Uber</strong> doesn't work there through the main app. There's an <strong>Uber Kazakhstan</strong>, but I couldn't install it; it's not available for Romanian users. Everyone uses <strong>Yandex</strong>, which is far more popular.</p>

<h2>Astana Weather: When to Go</h2>
<p><strong>Astana weather</strong> is extreme in the technical sense of the word. Summers run warm to hot, 25–35°C, with long days and very little rain. That's when I visited and it was ideal. <strong>Winter in Astana</strong> is another matter entirely: temperatures regularly drop to -30°C and the wind off the steppe makes it feel colder. The city functions perfectly in winter (it was built for it), but unless you're specifically curious about what -30 feels like, May through September is the obvious window.</p>
<p>Spring and autumn are brief and unpredictable. If you're planning around the weather, aim for June–August for outdoor exploration, or accept that in any other month you'll spend more time indoors than you planned.</p>

<p>A trip to <strong>Astana</strong> is surprising precisely because it's on no one's list; and the city is more interesting than you'd expect. 😊</p></div>
