<p><strong>Moscow, Russia</strong>: <strong>Red Square</strong>, <strong>the Kremlin</strong>, a boat ride on the <strong>Moskva</strong>, <strong>Novy Arbat</strong> &amp; <em>matryoshka</em>, and the famous metro stations with chandeliers.</p>

<h2>Red Square and the Kremlin in Moscow</h2>

<p>After two days on the train <strong>from Bucharest to Moscow</strong>, I woke up the next morning finally ready to explore the city. But first, I had to move to a cheaper hostel: grabbed my backpack, exchanged some cash, and headed straight there.</p>

<p>The catch was that finding the address turned into a real problem, because for some obscure reason Russians either don't number their buildings at all or number them in complete chaos. I walked in circles around the whole neighborhood for half an hour, asking anyone who would listen, and nobody could point me in the right direction, until a few girls spotted me and asked whether I happened to be looking for the <strong>Bananas</strong> hostel. "YES, of course I am!" "Spasiba!" 😊</p>

<p>The first stop of the day was the long-awaited, long-dreamed-of <strong>Red Square</strong>, which apparently gets its name not from its color but from the Russian adjective <em>krasnaya</em>, which means both beautiful and red.</p>

<p>As someone who was actually there and photographed myself in every tourist pose the square had to offer, I can say that the place really is red: the <strong>Kremlin</strong> wall is red, the museum at the far end is red, and <strong>Saint Basil's Cathedral</strong> is more red than green or whatever else people imagine it to be.</p>

<p>When I finally got there, all I could manage was a quiet "Oohh..." because everything was beautiful and unmistakably Russian, even if the center of the square was blocked off with bleachers being set up for a concert at the end of the month.</p>

<p><strong>Saint Basil's Cathedral, Moscow</strong>, with all its colorful little domes, struck me as surprisingly small, perched as it is on a small mound of earth beside the square, and strangely, it looks more beautiful from a distance than when you are standing right next to it.</p>

<p>From the square, you can walk along the <strong>Kremlin</strong> wall all the way to the other side, where the entrance is and where a park gives Muscovites somewhere to rest on sweltering days (meaning 29 degrees Celsius, which is the most heat I managed to catch there). Keep walking and you loop back into <strong>Red Square</strong>, now from the opposite end, for the classic <strong>Moscow</strong> shots with vendors selling <em>matryoshka</em> dolls, kites, and tourist excursions.</p>

<p>The <strong>Bolshoi Theatre</strong> sits close enough to <strong>Red Square</strong> that you'll walk past it anyway. Even without tickets, stop for the facade: neoclassical columns, <strong>Apollo</strong>'s quadriga on top, that particular Soviet grandeur <strong>Moscow</strong> does better than anywhere.</p> <p><strong>Bolshoi Ballet</strong> tickets sell out months in advance. Book before you travel, not once you arrive.</p>

<h2>What to See in Moscow: A Boat Ride on the Moskva</h2>

<p>I couldn't decide: visit <strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kremlin" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the Kremlin</a></strong> or take a boat ride on the <strong>Moskva</strong> River?</p>

<p>I had lined up for Kremlin tickets (<strong>350 rubles</strong>, about <strong>8.30 euros</strong> for the architectural ensemble), but in the end I slipped away rather shamelessly toward the water, bought a ticket (<strong>400 rubles</strong>, about <strong>10 euros</strong>), and spent three full hours riding back and forth along the river, largely because nobody bothered to kick me off once we reached either end.</p>

<p>This river of theirs, which shares its name with the city, is roughly the size of the Danube at <strong>Budapest</strong>, to give you a sense of scale. Boats are everywhere, though they all follow more or less the same route: from <strong>Kievskaya</strong> station (the one where trains from <strong>Kiev</strong> arrive, and by extension those from <strong>Bucharest</strong>), past the <strong>Kremlin</strong>, which looks fantastic from the water, all the way to a bridge just past the <strong>Rossia</strong> Hotel (that's what Russians call their country).</p>

<p>After three hours, drunk on sun and views, I walked back along the riverbank, past buildings in the style of <strong>Casa Scânteii</strong>, though they look far more impressive in <strong>Moscow</strong> than they ever did in the Soviet satellite states.</p>

<h2>About Moscow: Matryoshka Day</h2>

<p>Before setting off on the trip, I had been given two important missions by my mother, repeated several times to make sure they stuck: bring her back a <em>matryoshka</em> from Russia and send her a postcard of <strong>Saint Basil's Cathedral</strong>.</p>

<p>I decided to take care of both on my second day, and since I had found out that <strong>Arbat</strong> Street was the best place in all of <strong>Moscow</strong> for souvenir hunting, I headed there. And since I was going to that part of the city anyway, I wanted to walk along <strong>Novy Arbat</strong> as well, the boulevard brother to the old pedestrian <strong>Arbat</strong>.</p>

<p>First, I stopped at the post office (which goes by something close to "pochta" in Russian, so not too different from the Romanian word), where I asked for <em>dva shtamp Rumini</em>, meaning two stamps for Romania. The woman behind the counter asked for the postcard. I hadn't written it yet, so I said <em>niet</em> and walked on.</p>

<p><strong>Novy Arbat</strong> is a busy thoroughfare lined with newer buildings, tower blocks, capitalist advertising, and, from what I had read, the best clubs in <strong>Moscow</strong>. Walk to the end and you reach the river, but I turned off at some point toward old <strong>Arbat</strong>, which has nothing but small old buildings, plenty of tourists, a string of souvenir shops, street painters, and a relaxed, cheerful atmosphere.</p>

<p>I regretted not having more money to spend. There were Russian wool scarves and shawls everywhere, warm and beautiful, Russian fur hats and soft pelts, jewelry made from wood or amber, and sooo many <em>matryoshka</em> dolls, in every size, price range, and color imaginable.</p>

<p>If you don't know what <em>matryoshka</em> are, they are wooden dolls that contain smaller wooden dolls of the same design, each one fitting inside the next. The price goes up with the number of sisters nested inside. I saw some with 20 dolls and prices to match.</p>

<p>I wandered through the shops until my head spun. The <em>matryoshka</em> dolls seemed a bit expensive (I had seen cheaper ones near the <strong>Kremlin</strong>), the Russian scarves I could no longer look at without feeling a pang, and it was too hot for fur hats. I did pick up some postcards and a wooden brooch, and halfway down <strong>Arbat</strong> Street I stopped beside a statue and wrote the postcards. It was a clear, sunny day.</p>

<p>I walked back to the hostel via <strong>Red Square</strong>, the perfect place to waste time, and stopped inside the famous <strong>GUM</strong> department store, a grand shopping arcade carrying foreign goods where you can find absolutely anything.</p>

<p><strong>GUM</strong> spans the entire eastern side of <strong>Red Square</strong> under a glass and steel roof from the 1890s, the kind of structure that makes you look up and forget what you came in for. Mostly luxury brands now, but the Soviet-era ice cream counter in the main passage is still there and still has a queue.</p>

<p>On <strong>Pokrovka</strong> Street, a little further along, I had spotted two rats on my first morning in <strong>Moscow</strong>, near the gate of a building. One was thoroughly dead, and the other was spinning in dizzy circles around its own tail. Too much vodka is bad for your health?</p>

<h2>Visiting Moscow: The Metro with Chandeliers</h2>

<p>You have all probably come across those viral slideshows with photos of the Moscow metro: the luxury, the extravagance, the chandeliers... Do the Russians really have metro stations that look like ballrooms?!? Well... more or less, yes, but only a handful of the 182 <strong>Moscow</strong> Metro stations, not all of them.</p>

<p>Since my last day there was more about relaxing than rushing around, I set myself just two small goals: dropping in on old <strong>Lenin</strong> at home and visiting one of <strong>Moscow</strong>'s metro stations. <strong>Lenin</strong> didn't work out, because being on holiday, I woke up too late, and by the time I got to <strong>Red Square</strong> it was just past noon and they were no longer letting visitors in (it closes at 1 p.m.).</p>

<p>I headed for the metro. I had ridden their trains before, and the experience is very different from Bucharest's, though not because of the chandeliers but because of how deep the stations go. You descend as if into a mine, on escalators where you stand for minutes on end before reaching the platform.</p>

<p>At the bottom of the escalator sits a woman in a small booth with a row of monitors beside her, and if you somehow haven't paid for your metro ticket, she grabs you by the collar right there in front of everyone and sends you back up another endless escalator. At least, that's how I imagine it.</p>

<p>Back to the metro chandeliers: I had read that the stations worth visiting in <strong>Moscow</strong> were <strong>Komsomolskaya</strong> and <strong>Novoslobodskaya</strong> on the <em>ring</em> line, <strong>Kropotkinskaya</strong> on the red line, and <strong>Mayakovskaya</strong> on the green line.</p>

<p>At the first station, <strong>Komsomolskaya</strong>, I felt somewhat let down, as it struck me as fairly ordinary. The thing is, there are actually two connected stations, and the moment you reach the second one, you are greeted by something like the images below.</p>

<p>Of the four stations, only <strong>Kropotkinskaya</strong> failed to impress me, so I didn't bother photographing it. For <strong>28 rubles</strong> (<strong>0.70 euros</strong>), the price of a single metro ride, it's worth setting aside an hour or two to see these stations for yourself.</p>

<p>As a side note, in 2024 I visited the metro in <strong>Tashkent</strong> and liked their stations even more. The <em>design</em> is more creative and covers a wider range of themes.</p>

<p>One area worth a stroll if you have time is <strong>Moscow City</strong>, the skyscraper business district in the west of the city, a very different face of <strong>Moscow</strong> from the imperial center. Easy to reach by metro.</p>

<h2>The End of My Moscow Visit</h2>

<p>Two places worth the effort: <strong>Cafe Pushkin</strong> on <strong>Tverskaya</strong>, open 24 hours, staged like a 19th-century literary club complete with period menu. Theatrical, yes, but the food holds up.</p> 

<p><strong>White Rabbit</strong> occupies the 16th floor of the <strong>Smolensky Passage</strong> building, panoramic <strong>Moscow</strong> view, modern Russian cuisine. Not cheap. Worth it once.</p>

<p>I ended my visit to <strong>Moscow</strong> with a few unfinished wishes: to visit the <strong>Kremlin</strong> someday, which I had missed this time around, to see <strong>Lenin</strong>'s mausoleum, and maybe to reach a neighborhood on the outskirts and see for myself what the <em>real</em> <strong>Moscow</strong> looks like, the one where ordinary Russians actually live.</p>

<p>The next day I was heading to <strong>Saint Petersburg</strong> by train, continuing my journey through <strong>Russia</strong> and the Baltic states.</p>

<h2>Hotels in Moscow</h2>

<p>Back in 2011, I stayed cheap in <strong>Moscow</strong>, in a hostel, but today you'll find options for every budget, from a <em>chic</em> hotel in <strong>Moscow</strong> to <strong>Airbnb</strong> rentals.</p>

<p>If you want to stay in a historic <em>boutique</em> hotel in <strong>Moscow</strong>, here are three options:</p>
<ul>
 <li><strong>Metropol</strong> - An iconic hotel from 1905, with <em>Art Nouveau</em> architecture, located near <strong>Red Square</strong> and the <strong>Bolshoi</strong> Theatre.</li>
 <li><strong>Russo-Balt</strong> - An elegant hotel in an 1876 building near <strong>Arbat</strong>, with an aristocratic style and refined atmosphere.</li>
 <li><strong>Villa Kadashi</strong> - A historic villa converted into a <em>boutique</em> hotel, close to the <strong>Kremlin</strong>, ideal for an intimate and authentic experience.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Getting to Moscow</h2>

<p>I got to <strong>Moscow</strong> by train, but the most efficient option is to fly from <strong>Bucharest</strong> to <strong>Moscow</strong>. The <strong>Bucharest</strong> to <strong>Moscow</strong> flight takes about an hour, <strong>Aeroflot</strong> operates the route, and you can find <strong>Moscow to Bucharest</strong> tickets on <strong>Skyscanner</strong>.</p>

<p><strong>Moscow</strong> has three main international airports:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Sheremetyevo (SVO)</strong>, located northwest of the city, about 29 kilometers out</li>
  <li><strong>Domodedovo (DME)</strong>, to the south, about 42 kilometers out</li>
  <li><strong>Vnukovo (VKO)</strong>, to the southwest, about 28 kilometers out</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Moscow</strong>'s airports are connected to the city center by the <strong>Aeroexpress</strong> fast train, which gets you there in 35 to 45 minutes, stopping at:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Belorusskaya</strong> station from <strong>SVO</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Paveletskaya</strong> station from <strong>DME</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Kievskaya</strong> station from <strong>VKO</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Weather in Moscow</h2>

<p><strong>Moscow</strong> has a continental climate, with long, harsh winters where temperatures regularly drop below -10°C, and short but warm summers that can top 30°C.</p>

<p>I went in August and it was warm and pleasant.</p>

<h2>Moscow to Saint Petersburg</h2>

<p>The easiest way to travel from <strong>Moscow</strong> to <strong>Saint Petersburg</strong> is on the <strong>Sapsan</strong> high-speed train, which takes about three and a half hours and runs frequently from morning to evening. Tickets are available <em>online</em> and prices vary depending on class and how far in advance you book.</p>

<p>If you prefer something more classic, there are overnight trains like the <strong>Red Arrow</strong>, which has sleeping cars and is an experience in itself. For tickets and schedules, check the <strong>Russian Railways</strong> or <strong>Sapsan Trains</strong> <em>websites</em>.</p>

<p>I took the overnight train, left in the evening, and arrived in <strong>Saint Petersburg</strong> at sunrise.</p>
